1. What Are Carbon Credits?

A carbon credit is not a physical commodity. It is a digital entry in a registry — a serialized, uniquely identified unit representing one metric tonne of CO2 equivalent (tCO2e) that has been either avoided or removed from the atmosphere. Each credit carries a unique serial number, is linked to a specific project, and can be traced through its entire lifecycle from issuance to retirement.

Think of it like a land title in a cadastral registry: the value is not in a physical object you can hold, but in the registry entry itself and the institutional framework that gives it legal meaning. When a company "buys" carbon credits, it acquires the right to claim the associated emission reduction. When it "retires" credits, they are permanently delisted — the claim is consumed and cannot be resold.

The unit of measurement, tCO2e, standardizes different greenhouse gases. One tonne of methane, for example, is converted to its CO2 equivalent based on its global warming potential (approximately 28x that of CO2 over 100 years).

For legal counsel, this matters because contracts must be precise about what is being transacted. You are not selling tonnes of carbon. You are transferring registry entries with specific attributes — vintage year, methodology, project type, certification standard, and geographic origin. Each attribute affects price, quality perception, and regulatory acceptance.

2. History: From Kyoto to the Integrity Crisis

The Kyoto Protocol (1997) and CDM

The carbon credit concept was born with the Kyoto Protocol, adopted in 1997. The Clean Development Mechanism (CDM) allowed developed countries to meet their emission reduction targets by funding projects in developing countries. By the time the CDM wound down, it had registered over 8,000 projects and issued approximately 2 billion CERs — but many were of questionable quality. A Stockholm Environment Institute study estimated approximately 73% of CDM credits came from projects with low environmental integrity.

The Paris Agreement (2015)

The Paris Agreement changed the architecture. Every nation submits Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs). Article 6 governs carbon market mechanisms:

  • Article 6.2: Bilateral transfer of ITMOs (Internationally Transferred Mitigation Outcomes) between countries, requiring corresponding adjustments
  • Article 6.4: Centralized UN crediting mechanism (successor to CDM)

Glasgow Art. 6 Rules (2021)

Corresponding adjustments are the key concept: When credits are transferred internationally, the host country must add those emissions back to its own accounting to prevent double counting. Credits with corresponding adjustments can be used for compliance (e.g. CORSIA). The price premium is significant — potentially 2-3x the basic VCM price. Switzerland has been an early mover, signing 16 bilateral Article 6.2 agreements.

The Integrity Crisis (2023-2024)

In January 2023, The Guardian investigation claimed over 90% of Verra's REDD+ credits were "phantom credits." A Nature Communications 2024 study found 84% of 1 billion credits likely did not represent real reductions. Baselines were overstated by an average of 400%.

Specific cases:

  • Shell/Verra: ~960,000 invalid credits written off
  • Cambodia REDD+: No FPIC from indigenous communities
  • Brazil: Police raids on fraudulent Amazon carbon schemes

The crisis reshaped the market: generic avoidance credits collapsed to under USD 1/t. The market bifurcated into high-integrity and low-integrity segments. The ICVCM accelerated Core Carbon Principles. Rating agencies gained influence.

3. Market Size and Structure

Voluntary Carbon Market (VCM)

Valued at approximately USD 2.5 billion (2025). Projections: USD 15-47 billion by 2035. But the VCM represents only 1.4% of total carbon market value.

Compliance Markets

  • EU ETS: EUR 60-82/t, ~1.4 billion tonnes annually
  • China ETS: ~5 billion tonnes, ~EUR 12/t
  • California: USD 38/t
  • Canada: CAD 80/t → CAD 170/t by 2030
  • CORSIA: Mandatory for airlines from 2027

The 300x Price Spread

Generic REDD+ avoidance: ~USD 0.88/t. Direct Air Capture: USD 500+/t. Between these: cookstoves USD 3-8, community forestry USD 5-15, biochar USD 80-150. Price depends on: type (avoidance vs. removal), methodology, vintage, co-benefits, rating, and CCP eligibility.

A contract that simply references "carbon credits" without quality specifications is dangerously ambiguous.

4. Avoidance Credits vs. Removal Credits

Avoidance credits: Emissions prevented (e.g. forest protection). Based on counterfactual baseline. Challenge: baseline can never be verified with certainty → 400% overestimation problem.

Removal credits: CO2 actively taken from atmosphere. More direct measurement. Types: reforestation (biological), DAC (technological). Higher prices because:

  1. Measurement is more certain
  2. Permanence better assured
  3. Major buyers committed to removal-only (Microsoft, Stripe, Frontier)
  4. SBTi favors removals

For legal counsel: quality guarantees, pricing mechanisms, and replacement obligations differ fundamentally between avoidance and removal contracts.

5. Key Players Ecosystem

Standard-Setters and Registries

  • Verra/VCS: 2,000+ projects, 16% market share, 100+ methodologies. Damaged reputation, reforming.
  • Gold Standard: Geneva, WWF-founded 2003. SDG focus. CCP-mandatory Jan 2026.
  • Social Carbon: UK charity, 65+ projects. Only standard from Global South. NBS-exclusive. Hexagon Model.
  • Plan Vivo: Community forestry focus.
  • ACR: North America, California compliance eligible.

Project Developers

  • South Pole: Zürich, ~18% market share, 700+ projects, 50+ countries

Trading Platforms

  • Xpansiv/CBL: Dominant VCM exchange. GEO benchmark contracts.
  • AirCarbon Exchange: Singapore, strong in Asia.
  • Climate Impact X: Singapore, JV of DBS/SGX/Standard Chartered/Temasek.

Rating Agencies

  • Sylvera: London, USD 57M Series B. Satellite + ML ratings.
  • BeZero Carbon: AAA-to-D scale.
  • Calyx Global: ICROA-endorsed.
  • Pachama: Forestry, satellite imagery + AI.

Rating agencies are de facto regulators. A downgrade can trigger 30-50% price drops. Unlike VVBs (paid by developers), rating agencies are typically paid by buyers — reducing conflicts of interest.

6. ICVCM Core Carbon Principles

10 CCPs in three categories:

Governance (4): Effective governance, robust tracking, transparency, independent validation/verification

Impact (4): Additionality, permanence, robust quantification, no double counting

Sustainability (2): SD benefits/safeguards, net-zero transition contribution

98% of programs CCP-eligible. CCP premium: +25%. 30+ methodologies approved. Creating a two-class market.

7. VCMI Claims Code

Buyer-side integrity. Three tiers: Gold (on track, high-quality credits covering remaining emissions), Silver (committed, retiring credits), Bronze (beginning journey). The EU Green Claims Directive will likely align with VCMI, making compliance a regulatory necessity.

Key Takeaways for Legal Counsel

  1. Carbon credits are registry entries, not physical goods. Transfer of title occurs through registry booking.
  2. The 300x price spread demands precise quality specifications. "Carbon credits" alone is commercially meaningless.
  3. The integrity crisis created a two-class market. CCP-labeled credits command +25% premium.
  4. Compliance markets dwarf the VCM and set the price floor. EU ETS EUR 60-82/t, Canada rising to CAD 170/t.
  5. Rating agencies are de facto regulators. A downgrade can destroy credit value overnight.
  6. Article 6 corresponding adjustments change everything. Credits with CA can be 2-3x more valuable.
  7. The market evolves rapidly. Contracts need robust change-of-law provisions.
