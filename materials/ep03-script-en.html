EP03: Standards, Registries & the Integrity Revolution

Podcast Script (English) · ~3,600 words · Carbon Credit Market Masterclass

1. The Governance Paradox

Carbon credits underpin corporate climate claims worth billions of dollars, influence national climate strategies, and increasingly intersect with securities regulation and consumer protection law. Yet the entire system rests on private governance.

There is no government authority that "approves" a carbon credit. No securities regulator oversees issuance. No central bank guarantees their value. Instead, private standard-setting bodies define the rules, private registries track the assets, and private auditors verify the claims.

The closest analogies are credit rating agencies (Moody's, S&P, Fitch) or accounting standard-setters (IFRS Foundation) — private bodies whose determinations have enormous public consequences.

This structure has advantages: it is nimble and market-responsive. But it creates vulnerabilities: standards compete for market share (creating incentive pressure on stringency), accountability mechanisms are limited, and there is no lender of last resort if a standard-setter fails.

For legal counsel: every carbon credit contract is built on the foundation of a private standard-setter's rules — and changes to those rules can materially affect contractual obligations. Unlike statutory law, these rules can change through the standard-setter's own governance processes without legislative oversight.

2. Verra / Verified Carbon Standard (VCS)

Verra is the dominant force in the VCM. Registered in Washington, D.C. as a non-profit, Verra operates the Verified Carbon Standard (VCS), with over 2,000 projects and approximately 16% market share by volume. It maintains the broadest methodology library — over 100 methodologies covering renewable energy, waste management, forestry (REDD+, ARR, IFM), agriculture, industrial gases, and transport.

The REDD+ Scandal and Response

Verra was at the epicenter of the 2023 integrity crisis. The Guardian investigation alleged over 90% of REDD+ credits did not represent genuine emission reductions. CEO David Antonioli resigned mid-2023. Reforms followed:

  • VM0048 Methodology Revision: Jurisdictional (rather than project-level) baselines, improved deforestation modeling, more conservative crediting.
  • Enhanced VVB Requirements: Strengthened conflict-of-interest provisions and site visit protocols.
  • Registry Improvements: Better transparency and data access.

Verra's VCS program is CCP-eligible, meaning credits from approved methodologies can carry the CCP label — a significant commercial advantage with the emerging 25% price premium.

Despite reputational damage, Verra remains the standard counsel will most frequently encounter. Quality specifications should go beyond "VCS" — methodology, vintage, CCP eligibility, and rating agency assessment are all additional quality dimensions.

3. Gold Standard

Founded in 2003 by the World Wildlife Fund (WWF), headquartered in Geneva, Switzerland. Gold Standard differentiates through a dual emphasis: emission reductions plus sustainable development co-benefits aligned with the UN SDGs.

Projects must demonstrate measurable contributions to at least three SDGs beyond climate action (SDG 13). This requirement for verified co-benefits gives Gold Standard credits a reputation for higher social and environmental integrity.

Gold Standard is smaller than Verra but commands higher per-credit prices. Credits are prevalent in cookstove, water purification, and small-scale renewable energy projects.

CCP Mandate

Gold Standard committed to making CCP labeling mandatory for all new credit issuances from January 2026. This positions Gold Standard as the first major standard to require CCP compliance. All credits issued after that date carry CCP assurance by default.

For Swiss-based counsel, Gold Standard's Geneva headquarters and SDG co-benefit requirements provide additional contractual material — sellers can warrant specific development outcomes, and buyers can make more substantiated social impact claims.

4. Social Carbon Foundation

Registered as a UK charity with over 21 years of operational history and 65+ projects. The only major standard originating from the Global South — its foundations are rooted in Brazilian and Latin American experience.

Social Carbon is exclusively focused on Nature-Based Solutions (NBS). Unlike Verra and Gold Standard, it does not certify renewable energy, industrial gas, or technology-based projects.

The Hexagon Sustainability Model

Social Carbon's distinctive framework evaluates projects across six resource dimensions:

  1. Carbon — Core emission reduction or removal
  2. Biodiversity — Species diversity, habitat integrity, ecosystem function
  3. Natural Resources — Water, soil, natural systems
  4. Human Resources — Capacity building, education, skills
  5. Social Resources — Community cohesion, governance, cultural preservation
  6. Economic Resources — Livelihoods, income diversification, local economic development

SCD0003 Co-Development

Social Carbon co-developed the SCD0003 methodology module, integrating Eddy Covariance measurement technology — continuous, high-frequency measurement of actual CO2 fluxes between land surface and atmosphere.

Nature3 Initiative (July 2025)

Nature3 integrates carbon, biodiversity, and social impact credits into a unified framework, recognizing that NBS inherently delivers multiple benefit streams.

Article 6.2 Limitation

Social Carbon is currently NOT compatible with Article 6.2 of the Paris Agreement. Credits cannot be used as ITMOs and cannot benefit from corresponding adjustments. This is a material constraint for buyers needing credits that interface with national climate accounting.

5. Plan Vivo and ACR

Plan Vivo: The oldest carbon standard for community land use projects. Focuses on smallholder and community forestry. Smallest of the major standards by volume but respected for deep community engagement.

American Carbon Registry (ACR): Managed by Winrock International, focused on North America. Critical distinction: eligibility for the California Cap-and-Trade compliance market — one of the few bridges between voluntary standards and compliance market acceptance.

6. ICVCM Core Carbon Principles in Detail

The 10 CCPs in three categories:

Governance Principles (4)

  • CCP 1: Effective Governance. Board independence, stakeholder representation, grievance mechanisms, transparent decision-making.
  • CCP 2: Tracking. Registries must uniquely identify, record, and track credits to prevent double issuance.
  • CCP 3: Transparency. Project documentation, methodology details, validation/verification reports publicly available.
  • CCP 4: Robust Independent Third-Party Validation and Verification. Accredited VVBs with conflict-of-interest safeguards.

Impact Principles (4)

  • CCP 5: Additionality. Emission reductions must be additional — would not have occurred without carbon credit revenue.
  • CCP 6: Permanence. Adequate safeguards where reversal risk exists (Buffer Pool, insurance, monitoring).
  • CCP 7: Robust Quantification. Conservative assumptions, peer-reviewed science, credible data. Addresses the 400% baseline overestimation problem.
  • CCP 8: No Double Counting. No double-issuance, double-claiming, or double-use. Interfaces with Article 6 corresponding adjustments.

Sustainability Principles (2)

  • CCP 9: SD Benefits and Safeguards. Positive outcomes, no net harm, FPIC for indigenous communities.
  • CCP 10: Contribution to Net-Zero Transition. No lock-in of fossil fuel infrastructure.

98% of programs CCP-eligible. 30+ methodologies approved. CCP premium: +25%. Creating a bifurcated market.

7. AI-Powered Rating Agencies: The De Facto Regulators

Sylvera

London-based, USD 57 million Series B. Uses satellite data, machine learning, and domain expertise. Independently analyzes satellite imagery and deforestation data — a second opinion not paid by the developer.

BeZero Carbon

AAA-to-D scale, modeled on financial credit rating agencies. A downgrade can have immediate price impact — buyers with minimum rating thresholds may refuse delivery. Rating becomes a self-fulfilling prophecy affecting project viability.

Calyx Global

ICROA-endorsed. Provides project-level and portfolio-level assessments for large buyers.

Pachama

Specializes in forestry. Satellite imagery + AI to assess forest carbon stocks and monitor deforestation. Visual evidence makes quality assessment tangible for non-expert buyers.

Legal Implications

  • Price impact: A downgrade can reduce credit value by 30-50% overnight
  • Buyer contracts: Sophisticated buyers include minimum rating requirements
  • Market access: Some platforms require minimum ratings for listed credits
  • Due diligence standard: Rating reports are becoming standard due diligence

Unlike financial credit rating agencies, carbon credit raters are not yet subject to regulatory oversight (such as the EU CRA Regulation). This creates uncertainty about methodologies, liability, and accountability.

8. Registry Functions and Infrastructure

Registries perform essential functions:

  • Account Management: Creating/maintaining accounts, KYC procedures
  • Serialization: Assigning unique identifiers with embedded project attributes
  • Transaction Booking: Recording transfers, maintaining audit trails
  • Double-Counting Prevention: Credits cannot exist in two accounts simultaneously
  • Public Transparency: Searchable databases of projects, credits, retirements
  • Article 6 Interface: Interfacing with national registries for corresponding adjustments

The registry is the infrastructure layer — analogous to a stock exchange's central securities depository (CSD). This creates specific risks: downtime preventing transactions, fee changes affecting economics, rule changes affecting obligations, and no established migration mechanism if a registry operator fails.

9. The Swiss Carbon Market Ecosystem

KliK Foundation

Largest carbon credit buyer in Switzerland. Committed approximately CHF 2.5 billion through 2030. Established by the Swiss mineral oil industry to meet CO2 Act compensation obligations. Stringent quality requirements exceeding market standards.

South Pole

Zürich-headquartered, approximately 18% global market share, 700+ projects across 50+ countries. Near-collapse in late 2023 highlighted concentration risk in the developer landscape.

Gold Standard (Geneva)

Swiss headquarters means contractual disputes involving Gold Standard's rules could be subject to Swiss jurisdiction.

myclimate

Zürich-based non-profit foundation. Widely used by Swiss companies for voluntary offsetting.

BAFU as Article 6 Competent Authority

The Swiss Federal Office for the Environment (BAFU/FOEN) negotiates bilateral Article 6.2 agreements. Switzerland was an early mover, signing deals with Peru, Ghana, Senegal, Georgia, Dominica, Thailand, Vanuatu, and others. Credits qualifying under these agreements have higher commercial value.

Swiss Regulatory Environment

Switzerland's CO2 Act creates domestic demand through compensation obligations. FINMA has not yet classified carbon credits as financial instruments — but this question remains open.

Key Takeaways for Legal Counsel

  1. Carbon markets are governed by private standards, not public law. Every contractual reference to "certified" or "verified" credits rests on a private organization's rules. Contracts must address methodology revision risk and standard-setter governance risk.
  2. Verra dominates but carries reputational baggage. With 16% market share and 100+ methodologies, VCS credits are the most commonly traded. Contracts should specify methodology version, not just standard.
  3. Gold Standard's CCP mandate from January 2026 simplifies quality specifications. Credits issued after that date carry CCP assurance by default.
  4. Social Carbon's NBS specialization and SCD0003 co-development create unique positioning. However, the Article 6.2 incompatibility is a material limitation.
  5. Rating agencies are de facto market regulators without regulatory oversight. A downgrade can destroy credit value overnight. Contracts must address rating risk — which agency governs, minimum thresholds, post-delivery downgrades.
  6. The Swiss ecosystem provides a home-field advantage. KliK (CHF 2.5B buyer), South Pole (18% share), Gold Standard (Geneva HQ), and BAFU as Article 6 authority give Switzerland disproportionate influence.
  7. CCP eligibility is becoming the minimum quality threshold. With 98% of programs assessed and 30+ methodologies approved, CCP is becoming table stakes. Non-CCP credits face growing illiquidity risk.
