EP04: The Regulatory Tsunami — EU, CBAM, CSRD & Anti-Greenwashing

Podcast Script (English) · ~3,800 words · Carbon Credit Market Masterclass

Introduction: Why Regulation Matters More Than Markets

The carbon credit market is undergoing the most significant regulatory transformation in its 20-year history. Between 2024 and 2027, a cascade of new regulations — primarily originating from the EU but radiating globally — will fundamentally reshape which carbon credits have value, which claims companies can make, and what legal risks attach to every transaction.

A carbon credit sold today under a contract drafted without awareness of the Anti-Greenwashing Directive could become the basis for enforcement action in September 2026.

1. EU Emissions Trading System (EU ETS): The Anchor Market

Scale and Price

The EU ETS covers approximately 1.4 billion tonnes of CO2 equivalent annually — roughly 40% of total EU greenhouse gas emissions. It operates on a cap-and-trade basis: the EU sets an annual cap, distributes or auctions allowances (EUAs), and companies must surrender enough allowances to cover their actual emissions.

2025 prices: EUR 60-82/t. 2026 forecast: EUR 90-93/t. 2030 projection: EUR 149/t, driven by the accelerating cap reduction under the "Fit for 55" package (linear reduction factor increased from 2.2% to 4.3% per year starting 2024).

Swiss Linkage

Since January 1, 2020, the Swiss EHS has been linked to the EU ETS — the first and only full linkage with a non-EU country. For Swiss companies, this means EU ETS regulatory changes directly affect Swiss compliance obligations. There is no regulatory buffer.

How the EU ETS Works

The penalty for non-compliance is EUR 100/t — and the missing allowances must still be surrendered. This creates a hard price floor. The Market Stability Reserve (MSR) prevents price collapse by absorbing surplus allowances, which is why prices have stabilized in the EUR 60-80 range.

Contract Relevance

Contracts must distinguish clearly between compliance-grade instruments (EUAs) and voluntary market credits. The price differential — EUR 60-80 for compliance versus USD 5-10 for basic voluntary credits — reflects a fundamental difference in regulatory backing.

For Swiss companies, the EHS-EU ETS linkage means contracts referencing "applicable carbon pricing" must account for potential EU ETS price volatility. A contract signed at EUR 65 may face EUR 149 by 2030. Price escalation clauses are contractual necessities, not optional luxuries.

2. CBAM: The Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism

CBAM is arguably the single most consequential carbon regulation for global trade since the Kyoto Protocol. Definitive phase entered January 1, 2026, following a transitional reporting phase from October 2023.

What CBAM Does

CBAM imposes a carbon price on imports into the EU for six sectors: steel, aluminum, cement, fertilizer, electricity, and hydrogen. Importers must purchase CBAM certificates corresponding to the carbon price that would have been paid under the EU ETS. If the exporting country has its own carbon pricing, the importer can deduct the price already paid.

Non-compliance penalty: EUR 100/t of CO2 equivalent — deliberately set above the current EU ETS price.

Impact on Developing Countries

CBAM's most profound impact is on developing country exporters lacking domestic carbon pricing. Indonesian producers face a stark choice: either their government implements a carbon price, or their products become less competitive because importers must purchase full-price CBAM certificates. This creates a powerful incentive for every EU trading partner to establish credible carbon pricing.

Why CBAM Matters for Carbon Credits

CBAM does not directly create demand for voluntary credits — but it creates enormous indirect pressure. Countries establishing carbon pricing to avoid CBAM penalties simultaneously create domestic compliance markets. This expands regulated carbon, pushes up prices, and increases the premium for credits that can cross the compliance-voluntary boundary (as Article 6.2 ITMOs can).

A credit from a country with an active Article 6.2 bilateral agreement and corresponding adjustments has a fundamentally different value proposition than one from a country with no carbon pricing framework.

3. CSRD: The Data Demand Driver

The Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) does not directly regulate carbon credits but creates massive demand for verified carbon data.

Scope and Timeline

CSRD affects approximately 50,000 companies across the EU, phased in starting 2024. It requires reporting across Scope 1 (direct emissions), Scope 2 (purchased energy), and Scope 3 (value chain) emissions. Scope 3 — upstream and downstream across the entire value chain — is mandatory.

External Assurance

CSRD requires external assurance of sustainability reports, initially at "limited assurance" with progression toward "reasonable assurance." Companies cannot simply claim a carbon reduction — they must prove it to a standard that an external auditor will sign off on.

Contract Relevance

For a technology company providing aMRV (automated monitoring, reporting, and verification), CSRD is a tailwind. The 50,000 companies subject to CSRD need audit-grade documentation for their credit purchases. This creates a premium for technologically verified credits and a discount for credits based on manual or opaque methodologies.

Contracts should include data access and audit cooperation clauses anticipating CSRD assurance requirements.

4. EU Anti-Greenwashing Directive: The Credibility Crunch

The Empowering Consumers Directive — the Anti-Greenwashing Directive — becomes effective September 2026.

What It Prohibits

The directive bans generic environmental claims not substantiated by specific, verifiable, science-based evidence. These claims become illegal without detailed substantiation:

  • "Climate neutral"
  • "Carbon neutral"
  • "Eco-friendly"
  • "Green"
  • "Environmentally friendly"

A company claiming "carbon neutral" must demonstrate it has first reduced its own emissions in line with science-based targets and that any residual offsetting uses credits meeting specific quality standards.

Quality Requirements

In practice, credits must be certified under CCP or equivalent standards. Credits from registries that do not meet CCP-equivalent standards will not support defensible marketing claims in the EU.

Enforcement

Enforcement through national consumer protection authorities, with penalties of up to 4% of annual EU revenue. The enforcement mechanism is consumer protection law — well-established and active across EU member states.

Contract Implications

  1. Representations and Warranties: Any representation that a credit supports "carbon neutral" claims must specify the certification standard and confirm CCP-equivalent status.
  2. Indemnification: Buyers will demand indemnification against anti-greenwashing enforcement actions from credit quality deficiencies.
  3. Quality Specifications: Contracts must reference specific standards (CCP, Gold Standard, Verra VCS with additional certifications) rather than generic quality language.
  4. Disclosure Obligations: Credits for EU-facing use must come with documentation sufficient to substantiate claims under the directive's evidentiary standards.

5. CORSIA: Aviation's Carbon Compliance Mandate

The Carbon Offsetting and Reduction Scheme for International Aviation (CORSIA) is an ICAO mandate creating an entirely new compliance buyer class.

Structure and Timeline

  • Pilot phase (2021-2026): Voluntary participation
  • First compliance phase (2027-2035): Mandatory for states representing the majority of international aviation. Airlines must offset emissions above 2019 baselines.
  • Second phase (2036+): Broader mandatory participation

Demand Projections

CORSIA-driven demand: 1.6-3.3 billion credits over the compliance period. For context, the entire VCM transacts roughly 200-300 million credits annually. CORSIA could multiply total demand several times over.

Quality Requirements

CORSIA has its own eligibility criteria administered through ICAO's Technical Advisory Body (TAB). Not all voluntary credits qualify. The TAB has approved specific programs and methodologies, creating a premium quality tier.

The Demand Math

Current VCM volume: 200-300 million credits/year. CORSIA adds 1.6-3.3 billion over its compliance period. Even spread across a decade, this represents a 50-100% increase in annual demand. Supply cannot scale this quickly with high-integrity credits — CORSIA is structurally price-supportive.

Contract Relevance

Credits should be specified as "CORSIA-eligible" or "non-CORSIA-eligible." A CORSIA-eligible VCM credit can access both voluntary buyers and airline compliance buyers — a dual-market position commanding higher prices.

6. Broader Regulatory Landscape

Switzerland

CO2 levy of CHF 120/t on heating fuels — one of the highest globally. Net-zero by 2050, interim target 50% reduction by 2030 (split: 75% domestic, 25% international credits). KliK Foundation: ~CHF 2.5 billion to 2030.

Canada

Federal carbon price CAD 80/t (2025), legislated to rise to CAD 170/t by 2030 — one of the most aggressive jurisdictions globally.

China

World's largest ETS by covered emissions. Additional 3 billion tonnes added in 2025 (aluminum, steel, cement). Price ~USD 10-12/t but upward pressure expected as cap tightens.

7. The Six Megatrends of 2026

  1. Two-Class Market: CCP-certified credits command 25%+ premium. Gap widening as Anti-Greenwashing Directive approaches.
  2. CBAM Effect: Forces carbon pricing onto non-EU countries. No carbon pricing = lost trade competitiveness.
  3. Removals Boom: 62% of corporate buyers now prefer removal credits over avoidance credits.
  4. Article 6 Goes Real: 90+ bilateral arrangements, 176 pilot activities, actual ITMO transfers occurring.
  5. Anti-Greenwashing Enforcement: From September 2026, up to 4% of EU revenue as penalties. Imminent enforcement reality.
  6. China ETS Expansion: 3 billion tonnes added in 2025. Structural price direction is upward.

Key Takeaways for Legal Counsel

  1. The Anti-Greenwashing Directive (September 2026) is the most immediate contract risk. Every credit quality representation must be reviewed against the directive's evidentiary standards. Generic quality claims are a liability.
  2. CBAM creates urgency for compliance-grade credits. Countries without carbon pricing face trade penalties, creating demand for credits bridging the voluntary-compliance divide.
  3. CSRD drives demand for verified carbon data. 50,000 companies need audit-grade documentation for Scope 1/2/3 reporting, creating a premium for technologically verified credits.
  4. CORSIA introduces a new buyer class from 2027. Contracts should specify CORSIA eligibility where applicable — this designation affects pricing and buyer universe.
  5. Regulatory change clauses are no longer optional. The velocity of regulatory development means contracts must include specific provisions for changes affecting credit value, usability, or legal status.
  6. Swiss-EU ETS linkage means EU changes directly affect Swiss compliance. No regulatory buffer — EU ETS price movements, cap reductions, and rule changes flow through to Swiss obligations.
  7. The two-class market is structurally entrenched. CCP-certified and CORSIA-eligible credits occupy a premium tier; non-certified credits face accelerating demand decline.
