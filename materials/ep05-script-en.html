EP05: Article 6 Paris Agreement — The Game Changer

Podcast Script (English) · ~3,800 words · Carbon Credit Market Masterclass

1. Why Article 6 Changes Everything

For most of its existence, the voluntary carbon market operated in a parallel universe to government climate commitments. Companies bought credits voluntarily. Governments made pledges under the Paris Agreement. The two systems barely interacted. Article 6 of the Paris Agreement changes this fundamentally. It creates a legal bridge between voluntary carbon credit projects and sovereign climate commitments — transforming the economics, the legal architecture, and the strategic calculus of every carbon credit transaction.

For a legal counsel advising a technology company in this space, Article 6 is not an academic policy topic. It determines whether a carbon credit from a cookstove project in the DRC is worth USD 5 or USD 20+. It dictates whether a contract needs a simple bilateral structure or a multi-party architecture involving sovereign governments. It introduces a new category of risk — sovereign risk — into what was previously a purely private market.

2. The Paris Agreement Context

The Paris Agreement, adopted in 2015, brought together 196 parties — nearly every country on Earth — around a common climate framework. Unlike the Kyoto Protocol, which imposed binding targets only on developed countries, the Paris Agreement requires every party to submit Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs): self-defined climate targets that are periodically reviewed and ratcheted upward.

The gap between these pledges and actual action is enormous. Even if every country fully delivered on its current NDC, global warming would still likely exceed 2.5 degrees Celsius above pre-industrial levels — well above the Agreement's 1.5-degree aspiration. This gap is precisely why Article 6 matters: it creates mechanisms for countries to cooperate on emission reductions, allowing reductions to be achieved where they are cheapest and most impactful.

But cooperation between sovereign nations on emission reductions creates a fundamental accounting problem: if Country A pays for a reduction in Country B, who gets to count that reduction toward their climate target? Article 6 answers this question — and the answer has profound commercial consequences.

3. Article 6 Structure: Three Sub-Articles

Article 6.2: Bilateral and Multilateral Cooperative Approaches

Article 6.2 is the market-relevant mechanism. It allows countries to establish bilateral or multilateral agreements for transferring emission reductions between them. The units transferred are called Internationally Transferred Mitigation Outcomes (ITMOs).

Key characteristics:

  • Government-to-Government Framework: Two countries negotiate a bilateral agreement that sets the rules for how emission reductions generated in one country can be transferred to the other. The private sector operates within this framework but does not create it.
  • Scale and Momentum: As of early 2026, over 90 bilateral and multilateral arrangements exist globally. There are 176 pilot activities underway, of which 147 are under Japan's Joint Crediting Mechanism (JCM). 31 countries have published formal authorization policies, and 25 ITMO registries are operational or under development.
  • No UN Supervision Required: Unlike Article 6.4, Article 6.2 does not require UN oversight. The two cooperating countries set their own rules, methodologies, and quality standards — faster and more flexible, but also more variable in quality.
  • Corresponding Adjustments Are Mandatory: When an ITMO is transferred from Country A to Country B, Country A must apply a corresponding adjustment to its own NDC accounting — preventing double counting.

Article 6.4: The UN Centralized Mechanism (PACM)

Article 6.4 establishes a centralized, UN-supervised crediting mechanism — the Paris Agreement Crediting Mechanism (PACM), successor to the CDM.

  • UN Supervision: Operates under a UN Supervisory Body that approves methodologies, accredits verifiers, and oversees credit issuance (A6.4ERs).
  • CDM Transition: Of 7,800+ CDM projects, only 1,389 applied for PACM transition. Only 124 have been approved.
  • Delayed Timeline: A6.4ER credits are not expected until 2026 at the earliest. Article 6.2 is currently the only operational Article 6 mechanism generating actual transferable credits.
  • Global Recognition: When issued, A6.4ERs will carry the UN imprimatur — a significant credibility advantage.

Why CDM Failed — And Why It Matters

The Clean Development Mechanism (CDM) generated over 2 billion CERs but suffered from three structural flaws:

  1. Additionality gaming: The requirement that reductions would not have occurred without CDM revenue became a box-ticking exercise. Many projects, particularly in China's renewable energy sector, would have been built regardless.
  2. No double counting protection: The host country did not adjust its inventory when CERs were transferred. Both countries could claim the same reduction.
  3. Oversupply and price collapse: CER prices collapsed to near zero by 2012-2013, trading below EUR 0.50.

Article 6 was designed with these failures in mind. Corresponding Adjustments address double counting. The ICVCM's Core Carbon Principles address additionality. The bilateral nature of Article 6.2 prevents the open-floodgate oversupply that destroyed CDM pricing.

Article 6.8: Non-Market Approaches

Article 6.8 covers non-market approaches — capacity building, technology transfer — without trading emission reduction units. Important for climate policy but low direct commercial relevance for carbon credit transactions.

4. Corresponding Adjustments: The Key Concept

The Double Counting Problem

Without corresponding adjustments, international carbon credit transfers create a double counting problem. A Swiss-funded project in Ghana reduces emissions by 10,000 tonnes. Without adjustment, both Ghana (where the reduction occurred) and Switzerland (which paid for it) could claim the reduction. The atmosphere sees 10,000 tonnes reduced, but national accounts show 20,000 tonnes.

How Corresponding Adjustments Work

When Ghana transfers ITMOs to Switzerland under their Article 6.2 agreement, Ghana must add 10,000 tonnes back to its own emissions account. Ghana is saying: "We hosted this reduction, but we are transferring the climate benefit to Switzerland. We cannot count it toward our own NDC."

Consequences:

  1. The host country bears a real cost. By applying a corresponding adjustment, Ghana makes its own NDC harder to achieve.
  2. Credits with corresponding adjustments are worth more. ITMOs trade at USD 20+ versus USD 5-10 for basic VCM credits — a direct integrity-to-price translation.
  3. Host country authorization is required. A corresponding adjustment is a sovereign act, introducing sovereign risk into private transactions.

VCM Credits Without Corresponding Adjustments

Credits without corresponding adjustments remain legitimate private instruments — but they do NOT carry double-counting protection. Under emerging anti-greenwashing regulation, this distinction may become legally relevant: can a company claim "carbon neutral" based on credits that might be double-counted at the national level?

5. ITMOs: Internationally Transferred Mitigation Outcomes

ITMOs are not "credits" in the traditional VCM sense. They are authorized units of emission reduction approved for international transfer under an Article 6.2 bilateral agreement.

  • Sovereign authorization: Every ITMO requires formal authorization from the host country government.
  • Corresponding adjustment attached: Each transfer triggers a corresponding adjustment.
  • Price premium: ITMOs consistently trade at USD 20+, compared to USD 5-10 for basic VCM credits — a 2-4x premium.
  • Bilateral framework dependent: ITMOs can only be generated where a bilateral Article 6.2 agreement exists. No treaty means no ITMOs.

Host Country Authorization Process

The authorization process typically involves:

  1. Project registration with the host country's designated national authority (DNA)
  2. Methodology approval under the bilateral agreement's rules
  3. Verification of emission reductions by approved verifiers
  4. Formal government authorization of the specific ITMO transfer
  5. Registry recording of the transfer and corresponding adjustment

This introduces timeline risk, bureaucratic risk, and political risk. A project developer cannot guarantee ITMO issuance on a specific date because government authorization is a sovereign act not subject to private contractual control.

6. Switzerland's Article 6 Leadership

Switzerland is a global leader in Article 6.2 implementation, second only to Japan in bilateral agreements.

Bilateral Agreements (16 as of early 2026)

Switzerland has signed bilateral agreements with 16 countries:

  1. Peru (Oct 2020) — One of the first Article 6.2 agreements globally
  2. Ghana (Nov 2020) — 11,700 ITMOs transferred July 2025
  3. Senegal
  4. Georgia
  5. Vanuatu
  6. Dominica
  7. Thailand — First ITMO transfer to Switzerland in January 2024
  8. Ukraine — Paused due to conflict
  9. Morocco
  10. Malawi
  11. Uruguay
  12. Chile
  13. Tunisia
  14. Norway — First CDR-ITMO (Carbon Dioxide Removal ITMO) in June 2025
  15. Zambia
  16. Kenya

Institutional Framework

BAFU (Federal Office for the Environment) serves as the competent authority — negotiates bilateral agreements, approves projects, authorizes ITMO transfers. KliK Foundation is the operational buyer, channeling Swiss fuel importers' financial obligations into carbon credit purchases with a budget of approximately CHF 2.5 billion to 2030.

Switzerland's target — 50% emission reduction by 2030, with 25% achievable through international credits — creates structural demand for ITMOs. This is government-mandated acquisition with sovereign budget backing.

Key Milestone Transfers

Thailand to Switzerland (January 2024): Among the first ITMO transfers globally. Demonstrated the entire chain — project approval, verification, host country authorization, corresponding adjustment, registry transfer — actually works in practice.

Norway to Switzerland (June 2025): The world's first CDR-ITMO. Validates the removal pathway (not just avoidance) under Article 6, and demonstrates that even two developed countries see value in Article 6.2 cooperation.

7. The DRC Gap: Strategic Analysis

The DRC represents a critical strategic gap in Switzerland's Article 6 architecture.

Current status: There is NO Article 6.2 bilateral agreement between Switzerland and the DRC. The DRC is NOT among SECO's 13 priority countries for economic development cooperation.

Consequences without a bilateral agreement:

  • Credits can only be sold in the voluntary carbon market
  • No corresponding adjustments possible
  • No ITMO designation available
  • Credits valued at USD 5-10 rather than USD 20+
  • Increasing quality pressure under anti-greenwashing regulation

Strategic lever: Initiating a Switzerland-DRC bilateral agreement could transform economics — a 4x revenue multiplier from the same physical emission reductions. Treaty negotiation is a sovereign process requiring governmental engagement, but a private company can advocate for one and position its projects to benefit when a treaty materializes.

8. Article 6 and Registry Compatibility

Social Carbon is NOT compatible with Article 6.2 ITMO pathways. Credits registered under Social Carbon cannot be converted to ITMOs, structurally confining them to the voluntary market at VCM prices regardless of what bilateral agreements exist.

Verra (VCS) and Gold Standard have been more active in engaging with Article 6 requirements, though integration pathways remain works in progress. Registry selection is not just a technical decision — it is a strategic choice that determines whether a credit can access the ITMO premium pathway.

9. Contract Implications: Designing for Article 6

Dual-Pathway Architecture

Contracts should accommodate both VCM and Article 6.2 pathways for the same project. The same emission reduction can generate either a VCM credit (USD 5-10) or an ITMO (USD 20+), depending on bilateral agreement availability and host country authorization. Contracts must specify:

  • Which pathway is primary and which is fallback
  • What happens if the ITMO pathway becomes available after contract signing
  • Who bears the cost and risk of obtaining host country authorization
  • How pathway conversion is handled

Revenue Sharing: Pathway-Differentiated

If a credit is worth 4-5x more as an ITMO, the revenue sharing formula must account for this difference. A flat percentage split at USD 5 may not be appropriate at USD 25. Contracts should specify pathway-dependent percentages, ITMO pathway cost allocation, and who bears pathway conversion costs.

Host Country Authorization as Condition Precedent

Because host country authorization is a sovereign act outside private control, contracts should treat it as a condition precedent rather than a deliverable obligation. The contract must specify fallback to VCM pathway and pricing adjustments if authorization is denied or delayed.

Sovereign Risk Provisions

Article 6 introduces sovereign risk: refusal to authorize, delay, policy change, bilateral agreement revocation, political instability. Traditional force majeure and material adverse change clauses must be adapted to address these risks specifically.

Key Takeaways for Legal Counsel

  1. Article 6.2 is the operational pathway — Article 6.4 is delayed. Over 90 bilateral arrangements exist and actual ITMO transfers are occurring. Contract structures should focus on bilateral Article 6.2.
  2. Corresponding adjustments create the price premium. Credits with CA trade at USD 20+ versus USD 5-10 without. Every contract should specify whether CA apply and how they affect pricing.
  3. Host country authorization is a sovereign act, not a contractual deliverable. Treat it as a condition precedent with fallback pathways and pricing adjustments.
  4. Switzerland's 16 bilateral agreements create specific opportunities. Projects in countries with Swiss agreements can access ITMO pathways; projects without (notably the DRC) cannot.
  5. Registry selection determines Article 6 access. Social Carbon is not compatible with Article 6.2 ITMO pathways — a structural limitation affecting credit value.
  6. Dual-pathway contracts are the emerging standard. Same project, same data, but VCM and ITMO pathways with different prices, requirements, and risk profiles.
  7. Revenue sharing must be pathway-differentiated. A flat percentage across a 4-5x price difference will not adequately reflect costs, risks, and value contributions.
