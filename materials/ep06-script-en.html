EP06: The Great Convergence — When Voluntary Meets Compliance

Podcast Script (English) · ~3,900 words · Carbon Credit Market Masterclass

1. Two Markets Becoming One

For two decades, the carbon market has been split in two. On one side, compliance markets — government-regulated systems like the EU ETS where companies must surrender allowances for their emissions, with prices enforced by law. On the other side, the voluntary carbon market (VCM) — a private, unregulated space where companies buy credits because they want to, not because they must.

These two markets operated in almost complete isolation. Different buyers, different sellers, different prices, different rules. A compliance market allowance (EUR 60-80 per tonne) and a voluntary market credit (USD 5-10 per tonne) lived on different planets despite both nominally representing one tonne of CO2 equivalent.

That wall is coming down. Three converging forces — Article 6.2 ITMOs, CORSIA, and anti-greenwashing regulation — are dissolving the boundary. For a legal counsel drafting contracts in this space, this convergence is not a future possibility. It is happening now, and every contract written today must account for it.

2. The Historical Wall

Compliance Markets

Compliance markets are creatures of regulation. The EU ETS was established by EU directive. Switzerland's EHS is grounded in federal law. These markets exist because governments decided that emitting CO2 carries a cost.

Defining features of compliance market instruments:

  • Legally mandated (companies must hold them)
  • Supply is government-controlled
  • Quality is uniform (one EUA equals one EUA)
  • Prices are structurally supported by mandatory demand
  • Trading is regulated by financial market authorities

Voluntary Carbon Market

The voluntary market emerged bottom-up. Companies that wanted to offset emissions purchased credits from projects that reduced or removed CO2. No government mandated these purchases. Private standard-setting bodies (Verra, Gold Standard, Social Carbon) created methodologies, but participation was entirely voluntary.

Voluntary market characteristics:

  • Demand is discretionary
  • Supply is project-based and variable in quality
  • No government backing — quality depends on methodology and verification
  • Prices reflect willingness-to-pay rather than regulatory mandate
  • No financial market regulation (until recently)

The price gap — EUR 60-80 for compliance versus USD 5-10 for voluntary — reflected this structural difference.

3. Three Convergence Drivers

Driver 1: Article 6.2 ITMOs — Private Credits Become Sovereign Instruments

Article 6.2 creates a mechanism through which voluntary carbon credits can be transformed into sovereign instruments. A carbon credit project in Ghana reduces emissions. Without Article 6, this generates a VCM credit worth USD 5-10. With Article 6.2 and a bilateral agreement between Switzerland and Ghana, the same emission reduction can be authorized as an ITMO, transferred to Switzerland, and counted toward Switzerland's NDC. Ghana applies a corresponding adjustment.

The credit has crossed the wall. It started as a private voluntary market instrument and became a sovereign compliance instrument. Same physical reality — different legal and commercial status.

This is not theoretical. In July 2025, Ghana transferred 11,700 ITMOs to Switzerland. In January 2024, Thailand executed the first ITMO transfer to Switzerland. Price implication: USD 5-10 as VCM versus USD 20+ as ITMO. Same emission reduction. Different legal pathway. Different price.

Driver 2: CORSIA — Compliance Demand Enters the Voluntary Supply

CORSIA (Carbon Offsetting and Reduction Scheme for International Aviation) attacks the wall from the other direction. Instead of upgrading voluntary credits to compliance status, CORSIA channels compliance demand into the voluntary market's supply.

From 2027, airlines under CORSIA's first mandatory compliance phase must offset emissions above their 2019 baseline. Projected demand: 1.6 to 3.3 billion credits — dwarfing current VCM transaction volumes.

This creates CORSIA-eligible voluntary credits — credits from VCM projects approved by ICAO's Technical Advisory Body for CORSIA compliance use. Voluntary market credits meeting CORSIA standards gain a compliance-backed demand floor. Credits that do not meet standards lose access to this enormous demand pool.

Driver 3: Anti-Greenwashing — Regulation Enters the Voluntary Space

The EU's Empowering Consumers Directive (effective September 2026) effectively imposes regulatory standards on the voluntary market for the first time. Companies making environmental claims based on carbon credits must substantiate those claims with specific, verifiable, science-based evidence. Only credits meeting CCP-equivalent standards will support defensible claims.

Penalties: up to 4% of EU revenue. The voluntary market is no longer voluntary in the sense of being unregulated — it is now subject to consumer protection law enforcement.

The effect is a quality floor beneath the voluntary market. Credits that cannot meet CCP-equivalent standards face collapsing demand as buyers realize those credits carry regulatory risk rather than reputational benefit.

4. The Dual-Pathway Architecture

The convergence creates a new paradigm: the dual-pathway architecture. A single project, with a single monitoring system, generating a single stream of verified emission reductions, can produce credits along two fundamentally different pathways.

Pathway 1: Voluntary Carbon Market (VCM)

  • No state involvement required
  • Credit issued by private registry (Verra, Gold Standard, Social Carbon)
  • No corresponding adjustment — potential double counting
  • Price: USD 5-10 (basic), USD 10-15 (CCP-certified)
  • Faster issuance, lower transaction costs
  • Risk: anti-greenwashing pressure on non-certified credits

Pathway 2: Article 6.2 / ITMO

  • Requires bilateral treaty between host and acquiring country
  • Requires formal host country authorization (sovereign act)
  • Corresponding adjustment applied — no double counting
  • Price: USD 20+ per credit
  • Slower, higher transaction costs
  • Risk: government can block, delay, or revoke authorization

Same Data, Different Outcomes

The monitoring infrastructure is identical for both pathways. The same sensors measure the same emissions. The same data feeds the same reports. The difference is entirely legal and administrative — not technical. The technology investment is pathway-agnostic. But the commercial and legal architecture must accommodate both pathways and the possibility of switching between them.

5. Price Arbitrage and Its Contractual Implications

The same carbon reduction can generate:

  • USD 5 as a basic VCM credit
  • USD 10-15 as a CCP-certified VCM credit
  • USD 20+ as an ITMO with corresponding adjustment
  • Potentially more if CORSIA-eligible and purchased by an airline under compliance pressure

This is not a quality difference in the physical sense. The price difference is entirely a function of the legal and regulatory framework.

Contract drafting challenges:

  • Pricing Clauses: Fixed-price contracts are inadequate when the same credit can be worth USD 5 or USD 25. Contracts need pathway-specific pricing or price-sharing mechanisms.
  • Pathway Election Rights: Who decides which pathway a credit follows? The contract must specify election rights and criteria.
  • Conversion Rights: If a bilateral agreement subsequently opens the ITMO pathway, can the credit be converted? Who bears costs? Who captures the uplift?
  • Information Asymmetry: Contracts should address disclosure obligations regarding pathway-relevant developments.

6. Host Country Authorization as Bottleneck

The ITMO pathway's premium comes with a structural risk: dependence on host country government action. Risks include:

  • Refusal: Government may refuse for policy reasons — needing reductions for its own NDC.
  • Delay: Bureaucracies operate on their own timelines. Staff turnover, ministerial changes create delay risk.
  • Policy Change: Tightening criteria, imposing additional fees, or suspending the program.
  • Political Instability: Conflict or regime change preventing administrative action.
  • No Treaty: Without a bilateral agreement, there is no ITMO pathway regardless of project quality.

Contract provisions needed: condition precedent treatment, force majeure covering sovereign acts, VCM fallback provisions, realistic timeline expectations, MAC clauses covering policy changes.

7. Revenue Sharing: The 4x Problem

Consider a benefit-sharing structure of 50% community, 25% project developer, 25% technology/SPV:

At VCM pricing (USD 5/credit): SPV receives USD 1.25 brutto — potentially economically marginal after costs.

At ITMO pricing (USD 20/credit): SPV receives USD 5.00 brutto — economically attractive.

But should the percentage split remain the same? The ITMO pathway involves additional costs: government engagement, authorization fees, corresponding adjustment administration. Contracts must specify: pathway-independent or pathway-specific percentages, ITMO cost allocation, pathway development premium, and pathway election effects on revenue sharing.

8. The DRC Case Study: Convergence in Practice

Current state: No Article 6.2 bilateral agreement between Switzerland and the DRC. At VCM prices of USD 5-10 with 50/25/25 benefit-sharing, the SPV receives at most USD 2.50 brutto. After operational costs, the margin is thin or negative.

With a bilateral agreement: ITMOs at USD 20+. SPV share rises to USD 5.00+. Economics transform from marginal to viable.

Indonesia as contrast: Through Presidential Regulation 110/2025, Indonesia has comprehensive carbon market regulation. Article 6.2 agreements with Japan, South Korea, Singapore, and Norway. Switzerland in preparation. Projects in Indonesia can realistically plan for ITMO pathway access.

9. VCMI Claims Code: Buyer-Side Integrity

The VCMI Claims Code addresses how buyers can credibly communicate about carbon credit use:

  • Platinum: Achieved science-based targets AND uses high-quality credits for beyond-value-chain mitigation
  • Gold: On track for science-based targets AND uses high-quality credits
  • Silver: Committed to science-based targets AND uses high-quality credits

The common thread: emission reductions first, then credits. Companies cannot simply buy credits to claim climate action without demonstrating their own emission reduction efforts.

10. The Two-Class Market Accelerating

Premium Tier

Credits with one or more of: CCP-certified, CORSIA-eligible, Article 6.2 authorized (ITMO), removal-based, technology-verified (aMRV). Prices: USD 15-25+. Premium currently 25%+ over non-CCP credits and rising.

Standard Tier

Credits lacking these characteristics face: declining demand, price pressure, reputational risk, potential unmarketability under anti-greenwashing enforcement.

The Trajectory

The gap between tiers is widening. Every new regulation — CBAM, CSRD, Anti-Greenwashing, CORSIA — raises the quality bar. Credits that were "good enough" two years ago may not be marketable two years from now.

Key Takeaways for Legal Counsel

  1. The voluntary-compliance wall is dissolving. Article 6.2 ITMOs, CORSIA, and anti-greenwashing regulation are creating a single, stratified market. Contracts must accommodate both pathways and the transition between them.
  2. Same emission reduction, 4x price difference. The gap between VCM (USD 5-10) and ITMO (USD 20+) is a legal pathway difference, not a quality difference. Contracts need pathway-specific pricing and revenue sharing.
  3. Dual-pathway architecture is the new standard. Every carbon credit contract should contemplate both VCM and Article 6.2 pathways, with provisions for pathway election, conversion, and fallback.
  4. Host country authorization is sovereign risk. It cannot be warranted, only anticipated. Contracts need condition precedent structures, fallback provisions, and force majeure for sovereign acts.
  5. The two-class market is accelerating. CCP-certified, CORSIA-eligible, Article 6.2-authorized credits command premium prices. Non-certified credits face structural demand decline.
  6. Registry selection determines pathway access. Social Carbon's Article 6.2 incompatibility is a structural constraint. Registry choice is a strategic decision.
  7. The DRC-Switzerland bilateral gap is the highest-leverage strategic opportunity. Initiating this agreement could transform marginal VCM projects into viable ITMO projects with 4x revenue potential.
