EP07: Who Owns the Carbon? Rights, Consent & Benefit-Sharing

Podcast Script (English) · ~3,800 words · Carbon Credit Market Masterclass

1. What Are Carbon Rights?

Carbon rights are not a universally defined legal concept. There is no international treaty, no harmonized statute, and no consistent common-law doctrine that establishes what a "carbon right" is. Unlike mineral rights, water rights, or intellectual property rights, carbon rights do not emerge from centuries of legal tradition. They are a modern construct — contractually created, registry-dependent, and jurisdictionally variable.

When a client says "we own the carbon," the immediate legal question is: what exactly do you own, under which law, and who else has a claim?

In most jurisdictions, carbon rights are derived from a combination of:

  • Land tenure or concession agreements — the right to conduct activities on specific land
  • Contractual arrangements — agreements allocating the economic value of emission reductions
  • Registry rules — terms and conditions of registries (Verra, Gold Standard, sovereign registries) determining who holds serialized credit entries

None of these sources alone creates a complete "carbon right." A project developer may have a concession, a contract allocating emission reductions, and registry accounts holding serialized credits — but each is a separate legal instrument, governed by different rules, potentially subject to different jurisdictions.

A useful contrast is Australia, where the Carbon Credits (Carbon Farming Initiative) Act 2011 creates a statutory framework. Carbon sequestration rights can be registered on land title, separated from land ownership, and traded as a distinct property interest. Most developing countries — DRC, Indonesia, Cambodia — have not legislated this clarity.

The critical insight: carbon rights are not discovered or inherent. They are constructed through layered agreements, and gaps in those agreements create gaps in ownership.

2. Three Distinct Legal Concepts That Get Confused

Concept 1: Land Rights

Land rights determine who owns or controls the physical territory where a carbon project operates — freehold ownership, government concession, communal tenure, customary rights, or lease. In many carbon-relevant jurisdictions (DRC, Indonesia, Cambodia, Papua New Guinea), land tenure is contested, poorly documented, or subject to overlapping claims between formal state law and customary community rights.

Crucially, owning or controlling land does not automatically confer carbon rights. A landowner may have no right to the emission reductions generated on that land if those rights have been allocated to a government, concessionaire, or community under separate legislation.

Concept 2: Carbon Rights (Emission Reduction Rights)

Carbon rights — more precisely, emission reduction rights — determine who owns the climate benefit generated by specific activities on specific land. If a project prevents deforestation, who owns the avoided emissions? If a project plants trees, who owns the sequestered carbon?

The answer depends on national law (where it exists), contractual allocation (where national law is silent), and the specific project methodology.

Concept 3: Credit Rights (Registry Entry Ownership)

Credit rights determine who owns the serialized, numbered entries in a carbon credit registry. A Verra VCU has a serial number, vintage, project ID, and account holder. The account holder is recorded in the registry — but the account holder may not be the beneficial owner.

This is where technology companies operate. A company manages registry accounts on behalf of clients. The registry shows the company as account holder, but contractually, the credits belong to the client. This creates a custody-like arrangement that must be precisely documented.

The legal risk: in an insolvency scenario, poorly documented custody arrangements could result in credits being treated as the company's assets rather than client property. The financial services industry has decades of case law on this: segregation of client assets, clear custody documentation, regular reconciliation, independent audit. Carbon credit custody is functionally similar to securities custody.

3. The Three-Layer Consent Architecture

Layer 1: Contractual Consent (Company as Gatekeeper)

The service provider controls registry accounts. Credits are held in accounts managed by the company, even though they contractually belong to the client. This creates a gatekeeper function: the client owns the credits but cannot unilaterally transfer them without the company's cooperation.

The mechanism typically works through a Limited Power of Attorney (LPOA) embedded in the service agreement. The client authorizes the company to execute transfers subject to specific conditions — sanctions screening, counterparty checks, benefit-sharing compliance.

The legal subtlety: this creates both power and liability. Power to block transfers violating sanctions or benefit-sharing rules. Liability if the company refuses a legitimate transfer without contractual basis (breach-of-contract claims) or executes a transfer violating sanctions (criminal liability regardless of credit ownership).

Layer 2: Governmental Consent (Host Country Authorization)

For Article 6.2 credits (ITMOs), the host country government must authorize the international transfer and apply a corresponding adjustment. Without this, credits remain VCM instruments only.

The price differential is dramatic: Art. 6.2 credits at USD 20+ versus VCM-only at USD 5 — a 75% price reduction if authorization is denied. Host country authorization is a sovereign act that cannot be contractually guaranteed by a private party.

Critical contract gap: who bears the risk if authorization is denied? Who pays costs? Does the fee structure change? Does denial trigger termination rights?

Layer 3: Regulatory Consent (Benefit-Sharing and Compliance)

Mandatory benefit-sharing and compliance requirements take priority over private contractual arrangements.

DRC: The 50/25/25 Rule. Gross revenues from carbon credit sales are allocated: 50% to the national state, 25% to provincial governments and local communities, 25% to the investor/developer. Legal basis: Arrete interministeriel N°006/CAB/MIN/EDD/2023, building on Code Forestier 2002 (Loi N°011/2002, Article 89), operationalized February 2025.

Indonesia: Government Retention. The government retains a portion of carbon credit revenues under Perpres 110/2025 through the planned SRUK national registry.

The overarching principle: third-party benefit-sharing claims — from governments, communities, or indigenous peoples — take priority over private contracts. A contract that ignores benefit-sharing may be unenforceable.

4. FPIC — Free, Prior and Informed Consent

FPIC is not merely an ethical aspiration. In many jurisdictions, it is binding law. ILO Convention 169 (ratified by 24 countries) and the UN Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples (UNDRIP) establish FPIC as a right. National laws in DRC, Indonesia, and several Latin American states incorporate FPIC requirements.

Credits generated from projects lacking proper FPIC are legally and reputationally toxic.

The Isangi REDD+ Scandal

The Isangi REDD+ project in the DRC generated approximately 1.39 million credits, sold for more than USD 7 million. The facts:

  • Community consent was obtained five years after credit sales had commenced — the antithesis of "prior" consent
  • Annual payments to affected communities: USD 300 — a fraction of a percent of revenues. Even these were not made for seven years.
  • Promised infrastructure (schools, health facilities, roads) was never built
  • Severe reputational damage to all associated entities

Cambodia REDD+

REDD+ projects affecting 29 communities proceeded without FPIC. Reports documented forced evictions from project areas.

FPIC as a Contractual Safeguard

FPIC should be treated as a Condition Precedent in every client engagement involving land-based carbon projects:

  • Client must provide evidence of FPIC before services commence
  • FPIC documentation must meet minimum standards (community meetings, information in local languages, documented consent, ongoing engagement)
  • FPIC must be verified by an independent party, not self-certified
  • Ongoing FPIC compliance must be monitored throughout the project lifecycle

5. Carbon Land Grab — The Scale of the Problem

The term carbon land grab describes the acquisition or control of large land areas in developing countries primarily for carbon credit generation, often at the expense of local communities. The scale:

  • Global: More than 9 million hectares in carbon offset deals raising land grab concerns
  • Africa: More than 5.2 million hectares (RFUK report, October 2025)
  • DRC specifically: In January 2024, the DRC government annulled 8 carbon concessions covering 1.94 million hectares

Among the annulled concessions:

  • Tradelink SARL: 5 concessions covering 1.38 million hectares. Illegally awarded, exceeding the legal maximum of 500,000 hectares per concession under the Forest Code.
  • Safbois/Jadora: Annulled for illegitimate conversion of logging concessions and documented FPIC breaches.

A systemic audit found that only approximately 6% of 82 audited DRC concessions meet legality criteria. 94% have legal deficiencies.

6. Nine Recommended Contractual Safeguards

  1. FPIC as Condition Precedent — No services commence until FPIC documentation meeting defined minimum standards is provided and verified. Non-waivable.
  2. Area Verification — For DRC projects, verify concession areas do not exceed the 500,000-hectare maximum under the Forest Code.
  3. Independent FPIC Audit — Verification by independent local civil society organizations (in DRC: CODELT, OCEAN, CEDEN), not client-controlled entities.
  4. Community Grievance Mechanism — Functioning mechanism accessible to affected communities with documented response procedures.
  5. Ongoing Monitoring — FPIC and benefit-sharing compliance monitored throughout the project lifecycle.
  6. Termination Right for FPIC Breach — Immediate termination without compensation to the breaching client.
  7. Benefit-Sharing Compliance — For DRC projects, contractual confirmation that the 25% community share is being disbursed, with auditable evidence.
  8. Client Due Diligence History Check — Verify whether the client or principals have been involved in previous FPIC violations or concession annulments.
  9. Art. 6.2 Preference — Where feasible, structure projects under Article 6.2 frameworks, as state involvement adds an accountability layer.

7. Contract Gaps in Current Drafts

Five significant gaps that must be addressed:

Gap 1: Host Country Authorization Responsibility. Who is responsible for obtaining authorization under Article 6.2? This determines cost, effort, and risk allocation.

Gap 2: Authorization Denial Fallback. No mechanism for adjusting pricing, restructuring, or terminating if authorization is denied. The 75% price differential makes this a material economic risk.

Gap 3: Client Direct-Sale Rights. Unclear whether the client can sell credits directly to third parties without the company's involvement, and what compliance obligations attach.

Gap 4: Claims Liability Allocation. If a third party (community, government, NGO) brings a claim related to managed credits, defense costs and liability allocation between company and client is undefined.

Gap 5: Benefit-Sharing and Transfer Rights Linkage. Transfer rights are not tied to benefit-sharing compliance. The company should not execute transfers unless the client demonstrates compliance.

Key Takeaways for Legal Counsel

  1. Carbon rights are constructed, not inherent. Never assume a client "owns" carbon rights without verifying the full chain — land rights, emission reduction rights, and credit rights are three separate concepts requiring independent verification.
  2. Consent operates at three layers. Contractual consent (company as gatekeeper), governmental consent (host country authorization), and regulatory consent (benefit-sharing). Failure at any layer blocks the transaction.
  3. FPIC is non-negotiable. Make it a Condition Precedent. The Isangi scandal demonstrates what happens when FPIC is treated as a formality — USD 7 million in credits on the back of USD 300 annual community payments that were not even made.
  4. The DRC is a minefield. With 94% of audited concessions failing legality criteria, the default assumption for any DRC carbon project should be that legal deficiencies exist until proven otherwise.
  5. Address contract gaps now. Host country authorization responsibility, denial fallback, direct-sale rights, claims liability, and benefit-sharing linkage are all missing from current drafts.
  6. Association is liability. Being named as service provider to a project involved in carbon land grab creates reputational exposure regardless of contractual role. Robust onboarding safeguards are the only mitigation.
