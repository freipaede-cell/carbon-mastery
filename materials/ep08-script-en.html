EP08: Country Risk Deep Dive — DRC, Indonesia & Emerging Markets

Podcast Script (English) · ~3,900 words · Carbon Credit Market Masterclass

1. Why Carbon Projects Concentrate in High-Risk Jurisdictions

Carbon credit generation requires large forest areas (for REDD+ avoided deforestation), degraded land (for reforestation), or industrial facilities with high emissions baselines. The countries with the largest remaining forests — DRC, Brazil, Indonesia, Papua New Guinea, Peru, Colombia — are predominantly developing nations with governance challenges.

This creates a structural paradox: the jurisdictions with the greatest carbon credit potential are the jurisdictions with the greatest legal, political, and operational risk. A technology company cannot avoid high-risk jurisdictions without exiting the carbon credit market. The question is not whether to engage, but how to engage with adequate safeguards.

2. DRC Risk Profile — Seven Categories

The DRC represents the most complex risk environment in the global carbon market.

R1: Sanctions Risk — CRITICAL

The DRC is subject to four parallel sanctions regimes:

  • UN Security Council (UNSCR 1533, since 2004): 44 designated persons and 9 designated entities
  • US OFAC (EO 13413, since 2006): 20+ designated persons, 10+ entities. The 50%-rule means any entity owned 50%+ by a sanctioned person is itself sanctioned. Penalties up to USD 250,000 per transaction or twice the transaction value
  • EU Sanctions: 31 persons under DRC-specific regime plus 53 under related measures
  • Swiss SECO: Automatically adopts UN sanctions, selectively EU sanctions

Critical Development — March 2, 2026: The Rwanda Defence Forces (RDF) were sanctioned, reflecting the M23 conflict escalation. Direct implications for carbon projects in eastern DRC.

Under the Swiss Embargogesetz (EmbG), violations carry fines up to CHF 500,000 and imprisonment up to 5 years. Equipment such as drones, satellite communication devices, and GPS units could be classified as dual-use under sanctions regulations.

R2: Corruption Risk — CRITICAL

The DRC scores 20/100 on Transparency International's Corruption Perceptions Index. For Swiss-domiciled companies, the relevant framework is Art. 322septies StGB (bribery of foreign public officials).

The Glencore precedent is directly instructive: In 2024, Glencore was fined CHF 2 million by Swiss authorities and paid USD 150 million in the US for DRC-related corruption. "Wilful blindness" is not a defense under Swiss law. The 94% concession illegality rate identified by APEM/RFUK makes this a systemic risk.

R3: Tax Risk — HIGH

  • Withholding Tax: 14% WHT on service fees to non-residents. No DBA between Switzerland and DRC — no treaty relief
  • Permanent Establishment: 30-day threshold (vs. standard 183 days), triggering 30% corporate income tax
  • Carbon Tax (planned 2026): Details not yet finalized

R4: Counterparty Risk — HIGH

DRC projects typically operate through minimally capitalized SPVs. The DRC judiciary is widely assessed as corrupt and unreliable. The OHADA framework provides harmonized commercial law but creates legal uncertainty when interacting with DRC-specific carbon regulations.

The Congolese Franc (CDF) suffers from chronic instability — the central bank has only 11.8 weeks of import cover. De-dollarization since June 2024 creates currency inconvertibility risk.

R5: Regulatory Risk — HIGH

  • ARMCA Incomplete: Only 6 of 9 required regulations issued
  • Concession Annulments: January 2024 annulment of 8 concessions covering 1.94 million hectares
  • Benefit-Sharing: Mandatory 50/25/25 rule (50% state, 25% provinces/communities, 25% investor/developer)
  • No Art. 6.2 Framework: DRC credits remain VCM-only
  • REDD+ Overstatement: Tang et al. (Oct 2025) found ~400% overstatement globally

R6: Operational Risk — HIGH

M23 controls Goma and Bukavu as of January 2025. Eastern DRC is an active conflict zone. Infrastructure: 19% electricity access, 32% internet penetration. Equipment theft is documented; import duties average 12.6%.

R7: Reputational Risk — MEDIUM

The "carbon land grab" narrative is established in international media. Greenpeace, RFUK, and Pulitzer Center investigations have published detailed exposés of DRC carbon projects.

3. DRC Economic Reality — The 50/25/25 Waterfall

Illustrative Calculation (VCM-only): 1 million credits at USD 10/credit = USD 10 million gross.

  • State share (50%): USD 5 million
  • Provincial/community share (25%): USD 2.5 million
  • Investor/developer share (25%): USD 2.5 million gross — before carbon tax, operating costs, service fees, and 14% WHT

At VCM prices, the developer's net return could be minimal or negative. At USD 20+/credit with Art. 6.2 authorization, the same million credits generate USD 20 million gross — a fundamentally different proposition. Legal implication: do not onboard DRC projects that depend on VCM-only pricing.

4. Due Diligence Framework — 24 Questions

6 Showstoppers (Any "Yes" = No Engagement)

  1. Is any UBO on UN, OFAC, EU, or SECO sanctions lists?
  2. Evidence of corruption in concession award?
  3. Financial or operational links to armed groups?
  4. Project in active conflict zone where personnel safety cannot be ensured?
  5. FPIC not obtained or obtained after project activities commenced?
  6. Non-compliant with 50/25/25 benefit-sharing?

9 Risk Assessment Questions

Covering: concession size (>500,000 ha threshold), legal basis post-January 2024, corporate/SPV structure, UBO transparency, baseline methodology, projected credit volume, Art. 6.2 timeline, insurance coverage, and client track record.

9 Operational Questions

Covering: MRV methodology given infrastructure limitations, PE threshold management (30-day rule), equipment import/insurance/security, currency for in-country transactions, community engagement beyond FPIC, benefit-sharing audit trail, government relationships, conflict contingency plans, and exit strategy.

5. Twelve Contractual Risk Instruments

  1. Country Annex: Jurisdiction-specific annex identifying risks, requirements, and obligations
  2. Gross-Up Clause: Client bears all WHT — essential where no DBA exists
  3. Parent Company Guarantee: Required where contracting entity is a minimally capitalized SPV
  4. Compliance Representations: Sanctions, anti-corruption, FPIC, benefit-sharing — breach triggers termination and indemnification
  5. Conditions Precedent: FPIC documentation, concession legality, sanctions screening, benefit-sharing plan
  6. Sanctions Termination Right: Unilateral immediate termination if any party becomes sanctioned
  7. Anti-Corruption Clause: Aligned with Art. 322septies StGB, no facilitation payments, audit rights
  8. UBO Monitoring: Ongoing disclosure and screening
  9. Prepayment or Escrow: Mitigates counterparty credit risk
  10. FM/MAC Clause: Covers armed conflict, expropriation, sanctions, regulatory change, currency inconvertibility
  11. Equipment DAP: Delivered at Place — risk transfers to client at delivery point
  12. Flow-Down Provisions: All compliance, sanctions, termination provisions flow from FA to Work Orders

6. Indonesia — A Comparative Assessment

Indonesia presents a fundamentally different risk profile. Assessment: GO with conditions.

  • Regulatory Momentum: Perpres 110/2025 lifted the carbon credit export moratorium. SRUK national registry targeted for March 2026
  • International Integration: 5 MRAs signed (Verra, Gold Standard, Plan Vivo, GCC, Puro Earth)
  • Art. 6.2: Bilateral agreements with Japan, South Korea, Singapore, Norway. Switzerland in preparation
  • Tax: DBA provides 5% WHT on service fees (vs. DRC's 14%). PE risk at 60 days
  • Assessment: 7 mitigating measures, zero Showstoppers

7. Malaysia as Client Jurisdiction

Relevant as client domicile, not project host country.

  • Non-Compete: Section 28 of the Malaysian Contracts Act renders non-compete clauses ipso iure null and void
  • Tax: DBA provides 10% WHT. Offshore exemption may apply
  • Anti-Corruption: MACC Act Section 17A imposes corporate liability for corruption by associated persons

8. Bolivia — Emerging Market

June 2024: Constitutional Court overturned a 12-year ban on carbon credit trading. The Laconic deal (USD 1.2 billion) signaled commercial interest. RENAPP registry operational since March 2025. No Art. 6.2 framework, minimal regulatory certainty. Status: monitor, do not actively pursue.

9. Swiss Support Ecosystem

  • SIFEM: Swiss Investment Fund for Emerging Markets — DRC is a priority country
  • SERV: Swiss Export Risk Insurance — political, transfer, and counterparty risk coverage
  • KliK Foundation: Switzerland's primary ITMO buyer, seeking Art. 6.2 credits
  • S-GE: Market entry support and advisory

Important limitation: DRC is NOT a SECO priority country for bilateral development cooperation.

10. Multi-Jurisdictional Legal Comparison

CISG: 96 state parties. Carbon credits may qualify as "goods" — explicit opt-out required if unwanted.

Swiss OR Mandatory Provisions: OR 73 (5% default interest), OR 97-100 (liability for breach), OR 340 (non-compete max 3 years).

ICC Arbitration Costs: Three-member tribunal USD 200,000-500,000. Sole arbitrator USD 50,000-100,000.

Non-Compete Enforceability:

  • Switzerland: Max 3 years, scope/geography restrictions
  • California: VOID (BPC §16600)
  • DRC: Max 1 year
  • Malaysia: VOID (Contracts Act §28)

Key Takeaways for Legal Counsel

  1. DRC is the highest-risk jurisdiction in the carbon market. Four sanctions regimes, systemic corruption, 94% concession illegality, active armed conflict, incomplete regulation, and a benefit-sharing rule that may make projects unviable at VCM prices.
  2. The 50/25/25 waterfall changes project economics fundamentally. At VCM prices (USD 5-10/credit), the developer's 25% may not cover costs. Art. 6.2 authorization is essential — but the DRC has no framework yet.
  3. Indonesia is a GO with conditions. Positive regulatory momentum, international integration, manageable tax framework. Best risk-adjusted opportunity among major jurisdictions.
  4. Use the 24-question due diligence framework. Six Showstoppers are absolute red lines — any single finding terminates the assessment.
  5. Deploy all 12 contractual risk instruments. Country Annex, Gross-up, Parent Company Guarantee, and Sanctions Termination Right are non-negotiable for high-risk jurisdictions.
  6. Non-compete enforceability varies radically. Malaysia and California render them void. Switzerland limits to 3 years. Draft jurisdiction-specific provisions.
  7. Leverage Swiss support instruments. SIFEM, SERV, KliK, and S-GE provide risk mitigation — but DRC's non-priority status limits options.
  8. Sanctions monitoring must be continuous. The March 2, 2026 RDF designation shows rapid evolution. Implement real-time or monthly monitoring.
