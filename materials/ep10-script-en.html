EP10: Contracting Carbon — The Legal Counsel's Playbook

Podcast Script (English) · ~3,800 words · Carbon Credit Market Masterclass — Series Finale

1. The Multi-Party Structure — Who Is at the Table

The Core Triangle

  • COMPANY: Swiss-domiciled technology and advisory firm. Deploys aMRV technology, operates data platform, provides carbon accounting/compliance services, facilitates credit issuance. The integrator between hardware supplier and end client
  • CLIENT: Project owners worldwide — from private landowners to corporations to sovereign governments. A listed European corporate is a different counterparty than a DRC forestry SPV
  • MANUFACTURER: US-based LI-COR, manufacturer of Eddy Covariance Flux Towers. Critical supply chain dependency

The Expanded Structure in Emerging Markets

  • Government: Sovereign authority over carbon rights, benefit-sharing, project approvals (e.g., ARMCA in DRC)
  • Communities: Local and indigenous communities with customary land rights. FPIC is a prerequisite under major carbon standards. Failure to engage communities has resulted in project cancellations and legal challenges

Legal complexity scales nonlinearly. A five-party DRC structure requires managing government approvals, community consent, benefit-sharing, local counsel, and political dynamics — while maintaining contractual coherence.

2. The Contract Architecture — Four Layers

Layer 1: Framework Agreement (FA) — COMPANY-MANUFACTURER

The foundation document: equipment supply, IP ownership/licensing (COMPANY's analytics vs. MANUFACTURER's hardware IP vs. jointly developed components), revenue sharing, compliance obligations (export controls, sanctions, anti-corruption), warranty terms, tax indemnification, confidentiality, and non-compete.

Layer 2: Client Service Agreement (CSA) — COMPANY-CLIENT

  • CSA-A (Carbon Credit Model): Full-service engagement — carbon accounting + compliance (base layer) + complete carbon credit pathway (project design, MRV deployment, credit quantification, registry submission, monetization support)
  • CSA-B (Compliance Only): Standalone carbon accounting and reporting. Includes upgrade clause — supplemental agreement enables conversion to CSA-A without renegotiating the master agreement

Layer 3: Work Orders (WO) — Project-Specific Terms

Each project operates under a Work Order: project description, equipment specs, timeline, fee schedules, deliverables, project-specific SLAs, MANUFACTURER revenue sharing. Work Orders must align with registry requirements, methodology specifications, and jurisdiction-specific compliance.

Layer 4: Country Annexes — Jurisdiction-Specific Modifications

Country Annexes modify the CSA for specific jurisdictions. DRC: 12 risk mitigation measures. Indonesia: 7 measures. Country Annex provisions override conflicting CSA terms for the specified jurisdiction.

3. Infrastructure Options — Who Owns the Equipment

  1. None: Client owns compatible equipment. COMPANY provides software/analytics only. Rare in practice
  2. PaaS (Platform-as-a-Service): DEFAULT model. COMPANY procures from MANUFACTURER, deploys at client site, retains ownership. Client pays recurring PaaS Fee. Equipment returns on termination. Keeps equipment off client's balance sheet
  3. Lease: Third-party DFI or MANUFACTURER acts as lessor. COMPANY is explicitly NOT the lessor — critical for balance sheet treatment. Warranty covers entire lease term; replacement at MANUFACTURER cost

4. The 5-Fee Architecture

Fee 1: Structuring Fee — One-time. Both CSA-A and CSA-B. Project design, feasibility, PDD support, methodology selection. MANUFACTURER share: none.

Fee 2: Initiation Fee — One-time. Both CSA-A and CSA-B. Equipment installation, site preparation, calibration, commissioning. MANUFACTURER share: yes (shared per Work Order).

Fee 3: SaaS Fee — Recurring. Both CSA-A and CSA-B. Software platform, data analytics, continuous monitoring, reporting. MANUFACTURER share: none.

Fee 4: PaaS Fee — Recurring. CSA-A only (default infrastructure). Equipment-as-a-Service: use rights, maintenance, warranty, replacement. MANUFACTURER share: none directly.

Fee 5: Participation Fee — Performance-based. CSA-A only. Percentage of carbon credit revenue. Cash only — no in-kind credits. MANUFACTURER share: yes. Replaces artificial KPIs with market-based performance measurement.

Revenue Sharing: No default split. Each fee has a "Compensation" line in the applicable Work Order. If silent, MANUFACTURER receives 0%. In practice, Fees 2 and 5 are shared; Fees 1, 3, 4 remain entirely with COMPANY.

5. The Warranty Problem

MANUFACTURER's standard warranty covers only the "original purchaser" (COMPANY, not CLIENT). Warranty runs 24 months from shipment, not installation — 4-8 weeks transit reduces effective coverage to 20-22 months. No MANUFACTURER service network in emerging markets.

Solution — FA Article 12.4:

  1. Claim Acknowledgment: MANUFACTURER processes CLIENT claims as if from COMPANY
  2. Warranty Extension: 24 months from installation date (per commissioning report)
  3. Pass-Through Indemnification: MANUFACTURER indemnifies COMPANY for warranty-related costs at CLIENT sites

Under lease: warranty covers entire lease term, replacement at MANUFACTURER cost.

6. Country Annexes — DRC as Case Study

Twelve risk mitigation instruments for maximum-complexity jurisdictions:

  1. DRC Country Annex: Dedicated annex with 12 DRC-specific measures
  2. Gross-Up / Tax Indemnity: DRC imposes 14% WHT. Gross-up ensures COMPANY receives net amount
  3. Parent Company Guarantee: Required for minimally capitalized SPVs
  4. Compliance Representations: UBO disclosure, anti-corruption, sanctions compliance — conditions precedent to effectiveness
  5. Conditions Precedent: ARMCA registration, FPIC documentation, approved benefit-sharing plan
  6. Sanctions Termination Right: Immediate termination without penalty if any UBO appears on sanctions lists
  7. Anti-Corruption: Zero tolerance for facilitation payments. COMPANY audit rights. Incident reporting obligations
  8. UBO Monitoring: Quarterly confirmation. Automated SDN screening. Change-of-control notification
  9. Prepayment/Escrow: Fees in advance, held at international bank (not DRC-domiciled)
  10. FM/MAC Shortening: 90 days (vs. typical 180). After 90 days, either party may terminate. MAC covers regulatory, political, security changes
  11. Equipment DAP: COMPANY delivers to border/port. CLIENT handles customs, duties, inland transport
  12. Flow-Down: FA requirements (MANUFACTURER compliance, export controls, sanctions) flow into Work Orders

7. Multi-Jurisdictional Legal Framework

CISG

96 contracting states. Equipment purchases (US-Switzerland) are covered. Service agreements predominantly exempt (services >50% of value). Mixed contracts require careful analysis. Explicit opt-out required if unwanted.

Swiss OR — Mandatory Provisions

  • OR 73: 5% statutory default interest
  • OR 97-100: Full damages default. Cannot exclude liability for intentional/grossly negligent breach
  • OR 119: Impossibility releases both parties — could apply if host country revokes carbon rights
  • OR 163: Contractual penalties subject to judicial reduction
  • OR 340-340c: Non-competes must be reasonable — scope, duration, geography
  • OR 404: Mandate agreements are revocable at any time — mandatory, cannot be waived. If a court characterizes advisory services as mandate (Auftragsrecht), client can terminate at will. Distinguish mandate elements (revocable) from work contract elements (Werkvertrag, not freely revocable)

Dispute Resolution

  • ICC (3 arbitrators): USD 200,000-500,000+. 18-36 months. For disputes above USD 1M
  • ICC (sole arbitrator): USD 50,000-100,000. 12-18 months. Mid-value disputes
  • Expert Determination: USD 20,000-50,000. 3-6 months. Ideal for measurement/SLA disputes. Not for legal interpretation
  • Enforcement: New York Convention — 172 parties. Court judgments lack comparable enforcement infrastructure

Non-Compete Enforceability

  • Switzerland: Max 2-3 years, courts routinely reduce
  • California: Void per se (BPC §16600)
  • Delaware: Enforceable if reasonable
  • Malaysia: Void ipso iure (Contracts Act §28)
  • DRC: Max 1 year

Data Protection

  • GDPR: Max EUR 20 million or 4% global revenue
  • Swiss DSG: Max CHF 250,000 (against individuals, not entities)
  • Cross-border: Swiss-US Data Privacy Framework, SCCs, TIAs required

Force Majeure

  • Swiss OR 119: Symmetrical — both parties released
  • US Common Law: Purely contractual — exists only as defined in the contract
  • UCC 2-615: Asymmetrical — excuses only seller's performance

8. Open Decisions and Reserved Matters

Fifteen pricing and architectural decisions remain open: fee calibration, revenue-sharing ratios, escalation mechanics, operational thresholds.

Margin Protection is "[Reserved]" across all documents. The concept is decided (minimum margin mechanism); implementation remains open.

Tax Considerations

  • DRC: 14% WHT, no treaty relief
  • Indonesia: 5% WHT (reduced treaty rate)
  • Malaysia: 10% WHT (or 0% for offshore services)

PE risk exists wherever COMPANY deploys equipment for extended periods. A flux tower could constitute a "fixed place of business." Transfer Pricing documentation required for COMPANY-MANUFACTURER transactions — prepared by Big 4 or TP boutique, NOT a law firm.

Tax Indemnity (FA Art. 10.5(d)): IRS-imposed tax = MANUFACTURER's risk (territorial principle). COMPANY's risk = double taxation from inadequate TP documentation.

Key Takeaways for Legal Counsel — Series Wrap-Up

On Market Structure

  1. The VCM is transitioning from self-regulation to mandatory compliance. Every contract must anticipate regulatory tightening — EU Green Claims Directive (Sep 2026), Article 6.4, national regulations are scheduled realities.
  2. Carbon credits are NOT commodities. Each carries project-specific risk — methodology, permanence, additionality, political, verification. Contracts must reflect this heterogeneity.

On Standards and Compliance

  1. ICVCM Core Carbon Principles are becoming the integrity benchmark. CCP-eligible credits command premium prices; non-CCP credits face marginalization.
  2. CORSIA and EU CBAM create mandatory compliance demand. Compliance-eligible credits expand the addressable market — and intensify regulatory scrutiny.

On Technology

  1. aMRV provides a stronger evidentiary standard than activity-based estimation. The 1.5-1.6x accuracy improvement is a contractually significant differentiator.
  2. Blockchain secures data chain integrity but does not validate accuracy. Address calibration and error correction independently.
  3. SCD0003 closes the methodology gap for aMRV in NBS. Both competitive advantage and standard-setting responsibility. Protect contractually.

On Contract Architecture

  1. The four-layer structure (FA-CSA-WO-Country Annex) provides scalability. Resist flattening — complexity serves a purpose across jurisdictions and decades.
  2. The 5-Fee Architecture separates value creation stages. Cash-only Participation Fees simplify accounting, tax, and audit.
  3. Equipment warranty requires explicit intervention. Installation-date commencement, pass-through claims, and lease-term coverage must be negotiated.

On Jurisdiction Risk

  1. DRC = maximum-complexity scenario with 12 contractual instruments. This template scales down for lower-risk jurisdictions.
  2. Non-compete enforceability varies from "fully enforceable" to "void per se." Draft with severability and jurisdiction-specific carve-outs.
  3. OR 404 (mandate revocability) is a Swiss law trap. Minimize mandate characterization; maximize Werkvertrag characterization.

On Risk Management

  1. Export control classification is underappreciated. MANUFACTURER ECCN representations are essential.
  2. Cross-border data flows engage DSG, GDPR, and local sovereignty simultaneously. DPAs and TIAs must precede sensor activation.
  3. Fifteen open pricing decisions + "[Reserved]" Margin Protection represent both risk and flexibility.

The Final Principle

  1. Carbon credit contracting is infrastructure law for a new asset class. Contracts govern projects lasting 10-30 years through multiple regulatory cycles, technology generations, and market phases. Draft for durability. Build in amendment mechanisms, technology upgrade pathways, and regulatory adaptation clauses. The market in 2036 will look fundamentally different — and the contracts must survive the transformation.
